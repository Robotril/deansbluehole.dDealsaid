CPR for Drowning Emergencies

Rescue Breaths First
  1. 1

    Ensure the scene is safe

    Do not enter water if it puts you in danger. Bring the person to a safe, firm surface.

  2. 2

    Check for responsiveness

    Tap and shout: “Are you OK?” If unresponsive, proceed.

  3. 3

    Call 911 immediately

    Or direct someone else to call. Early activation is critical.

  4. 4

    Check for breathing

    Open airway. Look, listen, feel for max 10 seconds. Gasping is NOT normal breathing.

  5. 5

    Start CPR: Rescue Breaths FIRST

    Unlike cardiac arrest, drowning is oxygen deprivation. Give 2 rescue breaths before compressions. Ensure chest rises.

  6. 6

    Begin Chest Compressions

    • Push hard & fast in center of chest.
    • Depth: 2 inches (5 cm).
    • Rate: 100–120 bpm.
    • Cycle: 30 compressions → 2 breaths → repeat.

Duration

If you are alone and trained, provide CPR for 2 minutes before leaving to get help.

Continue until they breathe, a professional takes over, or you are exhausted.

AED Usage

Use an AED as soon as available. Turn it on and follow voice prompts.

Vomiting?

Common in drowning. Turn person to side, clear mouth, return to CPR immediately.

History of Dean's Blue Hole

The World's Deepest Blue Hole (Second Deepest)

Long Island, Bahamas

Formation & Geography

Blue holes are mysterious geological remnants of the Ice Age—vast, vertical sinkholes formed when ancient caverns in limestone collapsed and were later flooded as sea levels rose. Their striking dark-navy coloration, visible even from the air, comes from their extreme depth and narrow openings that plunge into expansive underwater voids.

Among the many blue holes found worldwide, few compare to the mesmerizing beauty and significance of Dean’s Blue Hole, located on Long Island in the Bahamas.

"At 202 meters (663 feet) deep, Dean’s Blue Hole is the second deepest known blue hole in the world."

Its entrance, a relatively modest 30 meters across, widens dramatically into a cavern nearly 100 meters in diameter, enclosed on three sides by a natural rock amphitheater and on the fourth by a brilliant turquoise lagoon.

Freediving & Records

Dean’s Blue Hole is famous as the annual home of the Vertical Blue Freediving Competition, one of the most prestigious events in the sport. Competitors travel from around the globe to witness world-class athletes attempt to reach staggering depths on a single breath.

The site has hosted numerous record-breaking dives, including William Trubridge’s finless descent to 92 meters in 2010. However, it has also seen tragedy, such as the loss of Nicholas Mevoli in 2013, underscoring the risks of the sport.

Today

Today, Dean’s Blue Hole remains a place of wonder—an irresistible blend of natural beauty, geological mystery, and human ambition. It invites adventurers to be bold but curious, respecting the techniques and safety practices essential for exploring such profound depths.

General Ocean Safety Tips

  • 1. Know conditions before entering:

    Check surf, tides, currents, and visibility. Avoid storms.

  • 2. Swim near a lifeguard:

    Most drownings occur on unguarded beaches.

  • 3. Understand Rip Currents:

    Look for choppy channels or foam moving seaward. If caught, swim parallel to shore.

  • 4. Don't swim alone:

    Always use the buddy system.

  • 5. Know your limits:

    Don't swim out farther than you can comfortably return.

  • 6. Enter water slowly:

    Avoid cold shock and check for drop-offs.

  • 7. Stay aware of marine life:

    Shuffle feet for stingrays. Don't touch coral.

  • 8. Avoid alcohol:

    It impairs judgment and reaction time.

  • 9. Wear appropriate gear:

    Fins, wetsuits, and bright colors for visibility.

  • 10. Protect against sun:

    Hydrate and use sunscreen.

Additional Safety for Blue Holes

Blue holes have unique hazards: extreme depth, vertical drops, unusual currents, and low visibility.

1. Advanced Environments Only strong swimmers/divers should enter deep sections.
2. Sudden Depth Changes Stay near edges unless trained.
3. Watch for Currents Avoid "siphon-like" currents at narrow openings.
4. Never Dive Alone Use a buddy who stays at surface if you go deep.
5. Monitor Buoyancy You can descend faster than expected.
6. Keep Exit in Sight Vertical walls can distract orientation.
7. Snorkeling Caution Stay above the rim; strong pulls can occur near center.
8. Avoid Center if Untrained Safest area is the outer shallow basin.
9. Temperature Shifts Watch for thermoclines and dark zones.
10. Respect Warnings Follow all posted signage and rules.

Diving & Snorkeling Safety

Freediving

Before Diving
  • Never freedive alone.
  • Rest and breathe normally; do NOT hyperventilate.
  • Check conditions.
  • Warm up gently.
During Dive
  • Equalize early and often.
  • Move slowly to save energy.
  • Know limits, turn back early.

Snorkeling

Safety
  • Stay within sight of shore.
  • Choose calm areas.
  • Use bright colors for visibility.
Technique
  • Relaxed breathing.
  • Avoid kicking sand or coral.

Scuba

Guidelines
  • Check gear thoroughly.
  • Review hand signals.
  • Monitor air and buddy.
  • Follow ascent rates strictly.
